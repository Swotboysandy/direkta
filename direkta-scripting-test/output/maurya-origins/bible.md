## 1 · Logline

When a scholar humiliated at the Nanda court swears an oath against the throne, he forges a beaten market boy — Chandragupta — into the leader of a hidden rebellion that must make Dhana Nanda's empire of fear collapse from within, or be crushed by it.

## 2 · Synopsis

**Short.** Under the seal of Magadha, Dhana Nanda's kingdom burns villages, seizes grain, and beats its people into lowered eyes. A boy who refuses to lower his and a scholar thrown down the palace steps find each other in the forest, and the scholar begins forging the boy into what the throne should fear. As whispers of a name reach the golden court, master and student look down on the capital at dawn and choose not to fight the empire — but to collapse it from within.

**Full (by trailer movement, present tense).**

**I — Tyranny (S1–S4, 0:00–0:52).** Over black, an old man's voice frames the world: kingdoms rise by sword, empires are born from suffering. Flashes — a burning village, grain dragged away in sacks, a crying mother, soldiers under Magadha's seal. In a crowded, hungry market, Nanda soldiers beat a farmer; the boy Chandragupta steps from the shadows in defiance, is slapped to the ground, and alone in the crowd does not lower his eyes. In a gold-drowned court, the scholar Chanakya warns the amused king that a throne built on fear cannot stand; the laughter dies. Thrown down the palace steps, scrolls scattered, blood at his lip, Chanakya ties his loosened hair into a knot and vows not to untie it until the Nandas are uprooted.

**II — Discovery (S5–S6, 0:52–1:20).** Soldiers chase Chandragupta through the forest at evening; he evades them on pure instinct until one corners him, and Chanakya's staff saves his life. That night, across a riverbank fire, the scholar weighs the boy's anger against his reason — and sees in his refusal of a kingdom the very thing that may deserve one.

**III — Forging (S7–S8, 1:20–1:47).** A training montage interleaves the making of a leader with the making of a movement: swordplay, maps drawn in dirt, falling and rising, stones placed on maps, villages whispering, rebels gathering, blades forged, Nanda spies watching from rooftops — while Chanakya's voice declares a king is made not by bloodline but by discipline, sacrifice, and the people who choose to follow him. In the Nanda court at night, a spy brings the king whispers that have a name; the golden cup stops halfway to Dhana Nanda's mouth.

**IV — Empire (S9–S12, 1:47–2:00).** At dawn on a hilltop above the vast capital, Chandragupta — no longer just a boy — asks how two men fight an empire; Chanakya answers that they will not fight it, they will make it collapse from within. A final montage: hidden banners raised, Nanda soldiers charging, a sword lifted against the sunrise. Cut to black. Title. Over black, Chanakya's voice promises that before a Maurya sits on the throne, a Nanda must fall.

## 3 · Tone & Themes  *(DRAFT — Pass 2)*

**Genre.** Historical epic origin saga (trailer format).

**Tone.** Hushed, grim slow-burn — the tension lives in silences (a court of "Gold everywhere. Excess. Silence." — S3; a cup stopping mid-air — S8) broken by short kinetic bursts (the chase, the montage, the charge). The feeling runs oppression-dark for a hundred seconds and permits hope only with the dawn — S9/S10: the rising sun, "A sword is lifted against the sunrise." Register per director (D3): Game-of-Thrones political dread at Gladiator scale.

**Themes.**
1. **Empires are born from suffering** — the VO thesis the whole trailer proves. — S1: "…but empires are born from suffering."
2. **Fear is an unstable foundation** — and by the end it changes sides. — S3: "A throne built on fear does not stand forever."; S8: "The cup stops halfway to Dhana Nanda's mouth."
3. **Kingship is earned by renunciation and consent, not blood.** — S6: "I don't want a kingdom." / "That is why you may deserve one."; S7: "A king is not made by bloodline… by the people who choose to follow him."
4. **Anger refined into strategy.** — S6: "Anger burns a man. Reason builds a kingdom."; S9: "We don't fight it. We make it collapse from within."
5. **The unlowered gaze** — defiance lives in the eyes. — S2: "The crowd lowers its eyes. But Chandragupta does not."; S4: "Close on his eyes."; S10: "Chanakya's eyes burn with purpose." (Also the visual-motif spine for §6.)

**Comparables.**
- **Gladiator** — the Hollywood grammar for ancient-world epic: intimate wrong escalated to imperial reckoning; scale shot with grit, not pageantry.
- **Game of Thrones** (director-set register, D3) — gold-drenched court decadence against mud-poor streets; power examined as machinery; dread carried in silences.
- **Batman Begins** — the origin-myth structure rhymes beat for beat: a mentor finds raw instinct (S5), reframes the wound into discipline (S6), a training montage mints a symbol (S7), and the enemy hears the name before the face (S8).
- *Anti-comparable (D3):* the saturated, theatrical Indian-TV mythological idiom — explicitly not this register.

**What makes it singular.** The empire-founder origin told as a two-hander: the sword and the mind forged into one weapon — and the throne is never confronted, only undermined ("We make it collapse from within" — S9).

## 4 · World / Setting

- **Place (on the page):** Bharat — S1: "In Bharat, kingdoms rise by sword…" — specifically the kingdom of Magadha and its capital — S9: "Below them: the vast capital of Magadha." Regime: the Nanda throne ("the Nandas," S4); the title promises the Maurya (S11–12).
- **Period:** c. 320–300 BCE, the Nanda→Maurya transition — DIRECTOR DECISION D2 (the page states no era; director chose "historically accurate"; wrote "300AD," logged as BCE — flag pending).
- **World rules (from the page):** the king claims total ownership — S2: "The king owns your land. Your grain. Your breath."; dissent is met in public and the public looks away — S2: "The crowd lowers its eyes."; the regime sees by spies — S7: "Nanda spies watching from rooftops."
- **Atmosphere:** streets "crowded, tense, hungry" (S2) vs. a court of gold, excess, silence (S3).
- **Design note (Pass 5):** the page gives no architectural or costume detail, and the chosen period (D2, c. 320–300 BCE) predates most surviving visual reference — production design will be inventing plausibly within the D3 register. Key looks need director sign-off before generation.

## 6 · Visual Language  *(DRAFT — Pass 3; proposals grounded in the page, director to tweak)*

**Aspect ratio [PROPOSAL]:** 2.39:1 widescreen — the epic frame the register (D3) implies; the hilltop vista (S9) and the market crowd (S2) want width.

**Palette.** The page carries its own color logic; grade it prestige-desaturated (D3):
- **Gold = the regime.** Hoarded, candle-warm, sickly — S3: "Gold everywhere. Excess. Silence."; S8: the golden cup. Gold appears *only* in court scenes and on regime bodies.
- **Dust = the people.** Greys, browns, undyed cloth, grain tones — S2: streets "crowded, tense, hungry."
- **Ember = the rebellion.** Fire-orange accents that change ownership across the trailer: tyranny's fire (burning village, S1) → teaching fire (riverbank, S6) → forging fire (blacksmith, S7).
- **Black → dawn.** The trailer literally travels from a black screen (S1) to a sunrise (S9–S10); the final dawn should be the first clean, warm, full-spectrum light in the piece.
- Red used surgically — blood at Chanakya's lip (S4).

**Lighting philosophy.** Single-source, naturalistic motivation (D3). The page splits time-of-day by allegiance — every tyranny scene is DAY (S2 market, S3 court, S4 steps: cruelty performed in public light), every conspiracy scene is NIGHT (S6 riverbank, S8 spy report). So: pitiless high sun for the regime's public stage; oil-lamp and candle glow pulling gold out of darkness in court; firelight on faces at the river; dusk backlight for the forest chase; and at S9 the dawn handover — low golden side-light as the world changes hands.

**Lens & camera.** Composed, static, symmetrical frames for power (the throne room: power is still); handheld urgency only where the page itself moves — S5: "He moves fast, instinctive, fearless."; S7: "Fast cuts." Low, reverent wides for scale — S9: "Below them: the vast capital of Magadha." Long-lens intimacy for the riverbank two-hander. And an extreme-close-up grammar for the eyes motif — the page scripts it: S4: "Close on his eyes."; S2: "The crowd lowers its eyes. But Chandragupta does not."; S10: "Chanakya's eyes burn with purpose."

**Editorial rhythm.** The page alternates held silences with flash bursts: black + VO then "Cut to flashes" (S1); "Hard cut" scene beats (S2–S4); "Fast cuts" (S7); an accelerating final montage to black (S10). Tempo map: 0:00–0:52 slow dread → 0:52–1:35 build → 1:35–1:47 the full-stop beat (the cup halts mid-air — let it hang in silence) → 1:47–2:00 surge and release → the final line over black as the button. Sound leads picture from frame one — S1: "Low, distant sound of marching feet. A faint chant rises."

**Recurring motifs.**
1. **The unlowered gaze** — eyes as defiance (S2, S4, S10).
2. **Gold vs. dust** — two worlds in one frame grammar; gold never touches the rebels.
3. **Fire changing ownership** — S1 → S6 → S7.
4. **The oath-knot** — tied at S4, visible in every subsequent Chanakya frame.
5. **Falling and rising** — slapped down but eyes up (S2); "Chanakya slowly rises" (S4); "falling, bleeding, rising again" (S7). The trailer's physical thesis.
6. **The arrested object** — the cup stopped halfway (S8): power interrupted mid-gesture.

- Stunts/SFX — the beating and slap (S2), chase choreography with spear/branch/trip (S5), staff disarm (S5), montage fight work (S7), horse work and battle charge (S10).

3. **S9's dawn** — a narrow magic-hour window for the emotional payoff shot (or its lighting simulation in generation).
4. **Period design without reference (D2)** — see §4 design note: plausible invention within the D3 register, sign-off before generation.

**Casting archetypes** — see the Pass 3 block below.
