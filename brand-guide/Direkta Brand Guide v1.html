Brand Guide — Master Document

Direkta is an AI-powered film production platform for directors and indie creators. Five workspaces. Nine specialised agents. One philosophy: you are the Director, the AI is your crew.

This document is the system of record for how Direkta looks, sounds, and moves. Every visual choice should feel like it was made by a director who knows exactly what they want.

01 / 08 ESSENCE

The Director's
Chair.

Direkta carries authority in its name. The brand must match — confident, direct, no decoration for decoration's sake. We elevate the creator's capability. We never gamify the craft.

Positioning Line

The director's chair for AI filmmaking.

One platform. Script to animatic. A full pre-production crew of nine specialised agents — and nothing ever ships without the Director's call.

Tagline

You direct.
AI delivers.

Operating Principle

Propose, don't commit.

Every agent shows options. The Director picks winners. Nothing saves without approval.

Personality — Five Traits
01
Confident
Speaks in declaratives. Never asks the user what they meant when it can ask one sharp question instead.
02
Precise
Filmmaker vocabulary, never generic. A "Cinematographer," not an "image generator." A "beat," not a "step."
03
Cinematic
Dark, wide, composed. The product should feel like walking onto a quiet, well-lit set.
04
Direct
No fluff. No hedging. No upsell language. Verbs first. The Director's time is the most expensive resource on set.
05
Crafted
Every detail considered. Direkta is for people who treat filmmaking as a craft — the product treats itself the same way.
Voice Rules

Five non-negotiables. If a sentence doesn't pass all five, rewrite it.

R—01
Speak Set
Use filmmaking vocabulary as the default. "Roll. Cut. Hold for picture. Slate." Generic tech words are a last resort.
R—02
Verbs First
Lead with the action. "Direct a Cinematographer." Not "Image generation tool."
R—03
Propose, Don't Decide
The agent surfaces options. The Director picks. "Here are six readings. Which one stays?"
R—04
No Hype
No "magic," no "revolutionary," no "unleash." We're a workplace, not a sales floor.
R—05
Respect the Craft
Never reduce filmmaking decisions to clicks. "Cast your lead" beats "Set up your character."

Construction

Geometry

  • Outer ring R = 92 / 200
  • Iris radius R = 72 / 200
  • Blades 6 — equal angle
  • Pupil offset +4 below centre
  • Stroke 3 unit @ 200u
  • Stroke join Miter — hard
  • Optical centre Y = 104

Why

The six-blade iris is the eye of the camera. The pupil sits below geometric centre — the same optical correction Bauer used on Univers. Hard miter joins on every blade. Nothing rounds. Cameras don't round.

Proportion Rule — 70 / 20 / 10
Ink — 70% Bone — 20% Tungsten — 10%

If tungsten exceeds 10% of any frame, something is wrong. The accent must remain rare to remain commanding.

Three Rules

01. No gradients. Not on backgrounds, not on type, not on buttons. The palette is flat or it is not Direkta.

02. No purple. No teal. No second accent. Tungsten stands alone.

03. Bone over white. Pure white reads as printer paper, not film stock. Bone is the standard.

05 / 08 ICONS

Drawn Like
A Set Plan.

Icons are linear, monoline, square-cornered. Each workspace gets one defining shape — the script page, the framed silhouette, the storyboard grid, the node graph, the export frame. Geometric, not illustrated. Honest, not cute.

WS — 01
Screenplay
Where the story lives.
The script page with a scene heading bar and indented dialogue. Reads as a written document, never as a paper-clip metaphor.
WS — 02
Casting
Soul ID · Visual identity.
A silhouette inside film-frame brackets. The brackets are the frame; the figure is who lives inside it. Never a smiling person.
WS — 03
Storyboard
Beat row · Variants picked.
A grid of frames with one filled — the picked winner. The right-most column is shorter — it suggests the next beat coming.
WS — 04
Stitch
Node graph · Animatic.
Three nodes, two directional connections. The metaphor is the editing suite — frames as nodes, clips as the lines between them.
WS — 05
Export
Frame · Deliverable out.
A film frame with content moving out and down to a baseplate. Suggests delivery — to disk, to pitch, to client. Never a cloud arrow.
RULE 01

1.5 px stroke at 24 px

Every icon is built on a 24-unit grid with a constant 1.5-unit stroke. Scale the icon, never the stroke ratio.

RULE 02

Hard corners only

Stroke-linecap and stroke-linejoin: miter. No rounded ends. No soft joins. Cameras don't round.

RULE 03

One fill per icon, max

If an icon needs to communicate a state — selected, active, generated — fill exactly one shape inside it. Two fills is too loud.

06 / 08 MOTION

Shutter.
Cut. Rack Focus.

Three motion primitives, all borrowed from a camera. Nothing floats. Nothing morphs. Nothing bounces. Hover any frame below to play.

M — 01

Shutter

180 ms · cubic-bezier(.55, 0, .1, 1)

Used when something appears or disappears decisively. Modals open. Panels close. Renders finish. Top and bottom converge on centre — like a mechanical shutter dropping.

Hover frame to play →
A
B
M — 02

Cut

0 ms · instant

Used for navigation, tab changes, beat-to-beat. No fade. No slide. No crossfade. The next state replaces the current one in a single frame. The most filmic transition there is.

Hover frame to cut →
M — 03

Rack Focus

480 ms · ease-out

Used when attention shifts within a frame — a tooltip activates, an agent is speaking, a selection is being committed. Background blurs, foreground sharpens. The reverse on release.

Hover frame to pull →
Forbidden Motion

No Float

Cards do not bob, hover-lift, or wiggle. They are placed on the desk.

No Morph

Shapes never tween into other shapes. A square that becomes a circle is a magic trick. We are not magicians.

No Bounce

Springy easing curves belong on consumer apps. Direkta is set-quiet.

No Confetti

No celebration animations. The work is the reward. A finished render is a fact, not a party.
